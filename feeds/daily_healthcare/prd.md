## PROLOGUE — ORIENTATION (Days 1–4)

**Day 1 · The $4.5 Trillion Question — Why Healthcare Is Different**
The U.S. spends ~$4.5T/year on healthcare — more per capita than any country — yet ranks poorly on outcomes. Three structural features explain why: the consumer is not the buyer (patient, provider, and payer are three separate parties with diverging incentives), demand is inelastic (you can't shop around during a heart attack), and information is asymmetric (the provider knows far more than the patient). The Iron Triangle of cost, quality, and access — and why every policy debate is a negotiation among those three corners.

**Day 2 · The Cast of Characters — Payers, Providers, Patients, and Everyone In Between**
A map of the five player categories: payers (commercial insurers, Medicare, Medicaid, MA, TRICARE, VA), providers (hospitals, physician groups, ASCs, FQHCs, post-acute, behavioral health, retail clinics), intermediaries (PBMs, clearinghouses, GPOs, HIEs, RCM companies), regulators (CMS, HHS, FDA, state agencies, DEA, OIG), and patients. Fully insured vs. self-insured plans. Why misidentifying the players means misunderstanding the incentives.

**Day 3 · Follow the Dollar — How Money Flows Through Healthcare**
The revenue cycle in miniature: premium → registration → encounter → coding → charge capture → claim submission → adjudication → payment → patient billing → denial follow-up. A ten-step relay race where every handoff is a chance for the baton to drop. Why the U.S. spends $250–$300B/year on billing administration alone. The 30–70 day timeline from visit to final dollar collected — and why it often stretches to 120+ days.

**Day 4 · Why the U.S. Is Different — A Global Anchor**
The U.S. is the only wealthy country without universal coverage, and understanding why is the prerequisite to understanding everything else. Per-capita spending roughly double the OECD average, yet life expectancy, maternal mortality, and amenable-mortality outcomes trail peers. How the multi-payer, employer-linked system emerged from WWII wage controls rather than deliberate design. Why drug prices diverge sharply from Europe and Japan — and how manufacturers arbitrage across markets. Why "just do what Canada does" analogies routinely mislead investors and policymakers: the U.S. system is path-dependent, politically entrenched, and structurally unlike anything else in the world.

## PART 1 — PAYER & INSURANCE (Days 5–24)

**Day 5 · What Is Health Insurance, Really?**
The concept of risk pooling. Premiums, deductibles, copays, coinsurance, and out-of-pocket maximums. Why insurance exists and the fundamental problem it solves.

**Day 6 · Commercial Insurance — Employer-Sponsored Coverage**
How most Americans get insured. Fully insured vs. self-insured (ASO) plans. ERISA and why self-funded plans play by different rules. The employer's role as purchaser.

**Day 7 · Plan Types — HMO, PPO, EPO, POS**
The alphabet soup of plan designs. Network restrictions, referral requirements, cost trade-offs. Why a PPO costs more but gives more freedom. In-network vs. out-of-network.

**Day 8 · Medicare Part A — Hospital Insurance**
Who qualifies, what it covers (inpatient, SNF, hospice, home health), how it's funded (payroll taxes, trust fund). The concept of benefit periods and spell of illness.

**Day 9 · Medicare Part B — Medical Insurance & Medigap**
Outpatient services, physician visits, preventive care, durable medical equipment. Monthly premiums, the Part B deductible, and the 80/20 split. Why that 20% coinsurance creates unlimited exposure — and how Medigap (Medicare Supplement) plans fill the gap. Medigap plan standardization (Plans A–N), guaranteed issue rights, and why ~28% of Original Medicare beneficiaries carry a supplement. Medigap vs. Medicare Advantage as competing approaches to managing Medicare's cost-sharing.

**Day 10 · Medicare Part C — Medicare Advantage**
Private plans that bundle A + B (and often D). How MA plans get paid via capitation. Star Ratings and their financial impact. Why MA is the fastest-growing segment.

**Day 11 · Medicare Part D — Prescription Drug Coverage**
Stand-alone drug plans and MA-PD plans. The coverage phases: deductible → initial coverage → catastrophic. Formulary tiers. How the Inflation Reduction Act reshaped Part D: the $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap, elimination of the coverage gap, the $35 insulin cap, and manufacturer discount obligations. What the traditional structure looked like vs. where it's heading.

**Day 12 · Medicaid — The State-Federal Partnership**
Who qualifies (income-based), what it covers, how states administer it differently. Managed Medicaid. The expansion under the ACA. FMAP and federal matching.

**Day 13 · Dual Eligibles — When Medicare Meets Medicaid**
The most complex and expensive population. D-SNPs (Dual Special Needs Plans). Coordination of benefits. Why this population accounts for outsized spending.

**Day 14 · TRICARE, VA, and Other Government Programs**
Military and veteran healthcare. CHIP for children. Indian Health Service. Workers' compensation as a payer. Dental, vision, and voluntary benefits (disability, critical illness, accident) as the ancillary carve-outs that ride alongside medical coverage but operate as separate products with separate economics. The patchwork of coverage.

**Day 15 · The ACA Marketplace — Individual & Family Plans**
Healthcare.gov, state exchanges, subsidies, essential health benefits, metal tiers (Bronze–Platinum). The individual mandate's rise and fall. Open enrollment.

**Day 16 · Enrollment, Eligibility & Benefits Verification**
The operational side: how a patient's coverage gets confirmed before care. Real-time eligibility checks (270/271 transactions). Why bad eligibility data causes downstream chaos.

**Day 17 · Prior Authorization & Utilization Management**
How payers control costs by requiring pre-approval. The PA workflow. Peer-to-peer reviews. Why providers hate it and why payers defend it. Recent reform efforts.

**Day 18 · Coordination of Benefits (COB)**
When a patient has more than one insurance. Primary vs. secondary payer rules. Birthday rule. How COB prevents overpayment — and creates confusion.

**Day 19 · Provider Contracting & Payer-Provider Negotiations**
How payers and providers negotiate reimbursement rates. Fee schedules, rate sheets, carve-outs, and escalation clauses. Network adequacy requirements. Contract term structures. Why contract negotiations shape the financial reality of every provider organization.

**Day 20 · Self-Insured Employers, TPAs & Stop-Loss**
Roughly two-thirds of commercially insured Americans are covered not by insurance at all, but by their employer acting as a self-funded plan sponsor under ERISA. How self-funded plans work: the employer bears the claims risk, a third-party administrator (TPA) or ASO carrier processes claims, and stop-loss insurance caps catastrophic exposure. Why ERISA preemption frees these plans from most state insurance regulation — and drives where innovation actually happens. The role of benefit consultants (Mercer, Aon, WTW) as gatekeepers of employer buying decisions. Direct contracting, centers of excellence, and reference-based pricing as self-funded strategies. Why this layer — invisible in most "insurance" coverage maps — is where employer purchasing power lives.

**Day 21 · Employer Point Solutions & The Benefits Navigator Stack**
Layered on top of every self-insured plan is a fast-growing ecosystem of point solutions and navigation companies that sit between the employee and the underlying medical benefit. Navigation and advocacy vendors (Accolade, Included Health, Transcarent, Quantum Health) help members find care, understand benefits, and steer to high-value providers. Condition-specific point solutions cluster by category: musculoskeletal (Hinge Health, Sword Health), cardiometabolic and diabetes (Virta, Omada), behavioral health (Lyra, Spring Health, Modern Health), fertility and family-building (Carrot, Maven, Progyny), cancer support (Color, AccessHope). The economics: PEPM fees, performance guarantees, ROI claims that rarely survive independent audit, and the integration burden on benefits teams. Why benefit consultants gatekeep adoption — and why this layer is where most digital-health venture capital has landed and where most of it is now being repriced.

**Day 22 · Payer Economics I — MLR & The Floor on Profit**
The single rule that shapes payer strategy more than any other. The Medical Loss Ratio: the ACA-imposed floor (80% individual/small group, 85% large group) that effectively caps payer profit margins and forces rebates when missed. How MLR is calculated, what counts as a quality improvement expense vs. administrative cost, and the games played at the boundary. State rate filing and Department of Insurance approval as the second gatekeeper on pricing. Why a payer that runs too efficient owes its members money — and how that single arithmetic constraint explains most of the sector's strategic moves toward vertical integration with providers, pharmacies, and care delivery.

**Day 23 · Payer Economics II — The Underwriting Cycle, IBNR & Risk Stabilizers**
The financial machinery underneath every health plan. The underwriting cycle: how rate adequacy swings between hard and soft markets over multi-year periods, and why payer earnings are cyclical even when membership is stable. Reserving and IBNR (incurred-but-not-reported) claims — why payer earnings are estimates until the claim tail runs out, and why prior period development is one of the most important footnotes in a payer 10-K. Risk corridors, reinsurance, and risk adjustment as the three ACA market stabilizers that keep the individual market from collapsing. Why payer financial performance is read through different metrics than any other industry.

**Day 24 · 🔄 Part 1 Review — Connecting the Payer Dots**
Recap and synthesis. A visual map of all payer types. Quiz yourself: given a patient profile, which payer(s) apply? How do they interact?

## PART 2 — PROVIDER & CLINICAL OPERATIONS (Days 25–43)

**Day 25 · The Care Ecosystem — A Provider Landscape Tour**
Hospitals, health systems, physician groups, FQHCs, ASCs, urgent care, retail clinics. How the delivery landscape is consolidating.

Inpatient vs. outpatient departments. Teaching hospitals, critical access hospitals, safety-net hospitals. How hospitals get paid differently by payer type.

**Day 27 · Ambulatory & Outpatient Care**
Clinics, physician offices, ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs). Why the shift from inpatient to outpatient is one of the biggest trends in healthcare. Site-of-service differentials — the same procedure paid very differently in a hospital outpatient department (HOPD) vs. an ASC vs. a physician office — are one of the most powerful investment and policy levers in the system, driving hospital acquisition of physician practices and the ongoing CMS site-neutral payment debate.

**Day 28 · Primary Care vs. Specialty Care**
The gatekeeper model. Referral patterns. Why primary care is considered the backbone of the system. The PCP shortage and its consequences.

**Day 29 · The Patient Journey — From Scheduling to Follow-Up**
A step-by-step walkthrough of a single patient encounter. What happens at each stage — and which systems, people, and data are involved.

**Day 30 · Clinical Documentation & Medical Records**
The chart, the note, and why documentation drives everything: coding, billing, quality, legal defense. SOAP notes. Structured vs. unstructured data.

**Day 31 · Care Teams & Clinical Roles**
Physicians, NPs, PAs, RNs, MAs, pharmacists, social workers, case managers. Scope of practice. Why "who can do what" varies by state.

**Day 32 · Healthcare Workforce & Labor Economics**
The nursing shortage, physician burnout, and staffing agency dynamics. Labor cost as the largest line item in healthcare operations. Graduate medical education (GME) funding. Scope-of-practice battles. Immigration and international recruitment. Why workforce is the industry's most pressing operational crisis.

**Day 33 · Credentialing & Privileging**
How providers get approved to practice and bill. The credentialing process with payers and hospitals. CAQH. Why it takes months and costs millions in admin.

**Day 34 · Post-Acute Care & Care Transitions**
What happens after the hospital stay. Skilled nursing facilities, inpatient rehab facilities, long-term acute care hospitals, home health agencies, and hospice — what each is, what each is paid under, and how patients move between them. The care transition process: discharge planning workflows, medication reconciliation at handoff, transition-of-care codes, and why handoff failures drive costly readmissions. The Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program (HRRP) and its financial penalties. Why post-acute is one of the most fragmented and operationally fragile segments of the delivery system.

**Day 35 · Long-Term Care & The Medicaid Spend-Down**
The custodial-care economy that sits outside the medical insurance system. Nursing homes, assisted living, memory care, and PACE programs. Why Medicare does not pay for long-term custodial care — and why most LTC is funded out-of-pocket until families spend down to Medicaid eligibility. Long-term care insurance: why the private market collapsed, what's left, and the hybrid life/LTC products replacing it. The Medicaid spend-down workflow, look-back periods, and estate recovery. One of healthcare's largest unsolved funding challenges, and a demographic time bomb as boomers age into needing it.

**Day 36 · Behavioral Health & Substance Use**
Mental health parity laws. How behavioral health is carved out or carved in. The access crisis. Integration with primary care.

**Day 37 · Telehealth & Virtual Care Delivery**
Synchronous vs. asynchronous. Licensure across state lines. Reimbursement evolution. COVID's lasting impact on adoption. Remote patient monitoring.

**Day 38 · Digital Health Business Models**
The venture-funded layer that has reshaped how care reaches patients — and where most healthcare investor attention now lives. Asynchronous telehealth and the messaging-first care model. Direct-to-consumer prescribing (Hims, Ro, Hers) and the cash-pay vs. insurance question. Virtual-first health plans (Firefly, Crossover) sold to employers as a primary care replacement. Hybrid in-person/virtual primary care (Forward, Carbon Health, One Medical). Care navigation and concierge models. The unit economics: CAC, retention, prescribing margin, insurance claims revenue, and the brutal transition from cash-pay growth to payer-contracted scale. Why most digital health companies stall at that transition — and where the durable winners are emerging.

**Day 39 · Health System Consolidation, Private Equity & Healthcare Finance**
Mergers, acquisitions, and why everyone is buying everyone. Hospitals acquiring physician groups, payers acquiring providers, retailers entering healthcare. Private equity in physician practices and its impact on care. Not-for-profit vs. for-profit economics. Hospital bond ratings and financial sustainability. The UnitedHealth/Optum vertical integration model.

**Day 40 · Reading Provider Financials**
> *Days 40–42 revisit concepts introduced across Parts 1–6 (MLR, PMPM, DRG, wRVU, 340B, corporate practice of medicine). Skim now for the framework, then return after Part 6 for the full payoff.*
How to read a non-profit hospital Form 990 and a for-profit system's 10-K. Operating margin vs. total margin, bad debt and charity care as separate lines, days cash on hand, and the leverage metrics bond raters actually watch (Moody's, Fitch, S&P health system criteria). 340B revenue disclosure, supplemental Medicaid payments, and the reliance on non-operating investment income that flatters margins in a good market and exposes them in a bad one. What to interrogate first when a system claims it can't afford a wage increase or a contract concession.

**Day 41 · Reading Payer, Physician & PE-Backed Financials**
Payer 10-Ks: MLR, PMPM revenue and cost, SG&A ratio, membership growth, prior period development, and segment breakouts across commercial, MA, Medicaid, and specialty. Physician practice and ASC P&Ls: productivity (wRVUs), collections per encounter, payer mix, and the operating leverage of fixed overhead. PE-backed rollups: EBITDA add-backs, platform vs. add-on economics, the management fee layer, and where the valuation math tends to break three years in. Which line items each stakeholder is trying to hide — and which footnotes actually tell you what's going on.

**Day 42 · Healthcare M&A, Valuation & Deal Structures**
The deal layer that drives the headlines. Strategic vs. financial buyers and the typical EBITDA multiples by sub-sector (health systems, MA plans, physician practices, RCM, healthtech, pharma services). Valuation methods: DCF, trading comps, precedent transactions, and the role of synergies (almost always overstated). Common structures: asset vs. stock deals, earn-outs, equity rollovers, and the MSO/friendly-PC structure required by corporate practice of medicine laws. Antitrust review under HSR, the FTC's recent posture on healthcare deals, and state-level merger oversight that has become a real gating factor. Why healthcare M&A has its own valuation logic — and the recurring patterns that produce write-downs three years later.

**Day 43 · 🔄 Part 2 Review — Mapping the Provider World**
Recap and synthesis. Trace a patient across multiple provider types. Connect provider operations to payer and payment concepts from Part 1.

## PART 3 — REVENUE CYCLE MANAGEMENT (Days 44–61)

**Day 44 · The Revenue Cycle — End to End**
The complete lifecycle: patient access → charge capture → claim submission → payment posting → A/R follow-up. Why RCM is a $100B+ industry.

**Day 45 · Patient Access — Registration & Scheduling**
Capturing demographics, insurance, and consent. Front-end revenue cycle. Why mistakes here cause problems months later.

**Day 46 · Insurance Verification & Financial Clearance**
Real-time eligibility. Benefits verification. Estimating patient responsibility upfront. Point-of-service collections.

**Day 47 · Medical Coding — ICD-10 Diagnosis Codes**
What ICD-10 is, how it's structured (chapters, categories, subcategories). Why specificity matters. The difference between coding for clinical care and coding for payment.

**Day 48 · Medical Coding — CPT & HCPCS Procedure Codes**
What was done to the patient. E/M levels, surgical codes, Category II & III. HCPCS Level II for supplies, DME, and drugs. Modifier basics.

**Day 49 · Coding Accuracy — DRGs, APCs, and Groupers**
How diagnosis and procedure codes get grouped for payment determination. The grouper engine: how it assigns MS-DRGs from diagnosis/procedure combinations, the role of CC/MCC lists, and why one additional secondary diagnosis can shift a case to a higher-paying DRG. APC assignment logic for outpatient services. Coding accuracy, clinical documentation improvement (CDI) programs, and the financial consequences of undergrouping and overcoding.

**Day 50 · Medicare Facility Payment — IPPS & OPPS**
How CMS calculates payment for hospitals. IPPS mechanics: base rates, MS-DRG relative weights, wage index adjustments, outlier payments, and the disproportionate share (DSH) and indirect medical education (IME) add-ons. OPPS mechanics: APC relative weights, the conversion factor, packaged services, and the device pass-through pathway. Why the wage index quietly drives billions in regional redistribution — and why hospital lobbying around it never stops.

**Day 51 · Physician Fee Schedule & Medicaid Rate-Setting**
The Medicare Physician Fee Schedule (MPFS): RVUs (work, practice expense, malpractice), geographic adjustments (GPCIs), and the conversion factor. The annual fight over the conversion factor and the budget-neutrality offset that pits specialties against each other inside the RUC. Medicaid managed care capitation rate-setting and the actuarial soundness requirement. How state rate decisions cascade into provider access. The payment math that turns codes and groups into dollars.

**Day 52 · Charge Capture & the Chargemaster**
How services get translated into billable charges. The hospital chargemaster. Charge description master (CDM) maintenance. Revenue codes.

**Day 53 · Claim Forms — UB-04 vs. CMS-1500**
Institutional claims (UB-04) for facilities vs. professional claims (CMS-1500) for physicians. Key fields and what they mean. When each is used.

**Day 54 · EDI in the Revenue Cycle — The Electronic Claim Lifecycle**
How electronic data interchange powers claims operations. The key transaction sets in action: 837 (claim submission), 835 (remittance), 270/271 (eligibility), 276/277 (claim status), and 278 (authorization). How clearinghouses sit between providers and payers — translating formats, validating structure, and routing transactions. The role of trading partner agreements and enrollment. How electronic connectivity replaced paper and why it matters for speed-to-payment.

**Day 55 · Claims Submission & Scrubbing**
Clean claims vs. dirty claims — and why the distinction drives cash flow. Claim scrubbing engines and the edits they run: NCCI edits (bundling rules), MUE limits, LCD/NCD medical necessity checks, and payer-specific rules. Front-end rejections vs. back-end denials. First-pass acceptance rates as a key RCM metric. How scrubbing rules are maintained, why they change with each CMS update cycle, and the cost of letting errors through.

**Day 56 · Adjudication — How Payers Process Claims**
What happens inside the payer: eligibility check → benefit verification → medical policy → pricing → payment determination. Auto-adjudication vs. manual review.

**Day 57 · Remittance & Payment Posting — ERA/EOB**
The 835 transaction. Reading an Explanation of Benefits. Allowed amount, contractual adjustment, patient responsibility. Reconciling payments.

**Day 58 · Denial Management — The Revenue Killer**
Common denial reasons (eligibility, authorization, coding, timely filing). Denial categories: hard vs. soft. Prevention vs. recovery. The cost of rework.

**Day 59 · Appeals — Fighting for Payment**
First-level, second-level, and external appeals. Documentation requirements. Timelines. When to appeal vs. when to write off. Payer-specific nuances.

**Day 60 · Patient Billing, Statements & Collections**
The patient as the "last payer." Statement cycles. Payment plans. Bad debt vs. charity care. The No Surprises Act and surprise billing protections — and the federal independent dispute resolution (IDR) process where payers and out-of-network providers now fight over the actual reimbursement amount, which has become the real battleground that determines whether the law saves money or just shifts it.

**Day 61 · 🔄 Part 3 Review — Following a Claim from Birth to Payment**
Recap and synthesis. Trace a single encounter through every RCM step. Identify where breakdowns happen and how they cascade.

**Day 73 · CMS Innovation Center (CMMI) Models**

**Day 74 · Commercial VBC — Payer-Provider Contracts**

**Day 75 · VBC Enablers & Risk-Bearing Primary Care**
The companies built specifically to take downside risk on Medicare populations — the single most investable VBC sub-sector of the past decade. The MA full-risk primary care model: Oak Street, ChenMed, CenterWell (Humana), and Iora/One Medical Senior. The MSO/enabler model: Agilon Health, Privia Health, and Aledade — providing the capital, infrastructure, payer contracts, and analytics that let independent physicians enter risk arrangements without rebuilding from scratch. **Day 76 · Social Determinants in Value-Based Care**
Why addressing housing, food, transportation matters for outcomes and total cost. Z-codes. Community health worker models. The investment case.

**Day 91 · Pharmacy Compliance — DEA, DSCSA, State Boards**
Controlled substance scheduling. The Drug Supply Chain Security Act and track-and-trace. State pharmacy board regulations. Pharmacist scope of practice.

**Day 92 · GLP-1s & The Obesity Drug Cost Story**
The single largest cost story currently reshaping U.S. healthcare. Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound): mechanism, indications, and why diabetes-only coverage has spilled into broader obesity demand. The list price vs. net price gap, employer plan exclusions, prior authorization gates, and the rise of cash-pay channels. Compounded GLP-1s and the FDA shortage list. Long-term cardiometabolic outcome trials and the value-based pricing arguments. Why a single drug class can move a self-insured employer's plan trend by several percentage points — and the second-order impact on bariatric surgery volumes, cardiometabolic point solutions, and even consumer-staples equities.

**Day 93 · Medical Devices & the FDA Device Pathway**
The device industry is ~$200B+ and structurally different from pharma. The landscape: Medtronic, J&J, Stryker, Abbott, Boston Scientific, BD, and a long tail of single-product companies. FDA pathways: 510(k) for "substantial equivalence" (the vast majority), PMA for high-risk Class III devices, De Novo for novel low- and moderate-risk devices, and the HDE humanitarian pathway. Why 510(k) is both the engine of device innovation and its most criticized feature. Implantables vs. capital equipment vs. consumables — three very different business models. GPO contracting and physician-preference items. How site of service (HOPD vs. ASC vs. office) reshapes device economics and who captures the margin.

**Day 94 · Diagnostics & Lab Economics**
The testing layer most financial analysts overlook until they have to price it. The LabCorp/Quest duopoly in reference labs, hospital outreach labs competing at the margins, and physician-office labs. CLIA certification as the regulatory floor for any lab testing. IVD (FDA-cleared in-vitro diagnostic kits) vs. LDT (laboratory-developed tests) and the long-running FDA jurisdiction fight. Reimbursement: the Clinical Lab Fee Schedule (CLFS), PAMA-driven rate resets, and why routine lab rates have collapsed. Molecular diagnostics, genomics, and liquid biopsy as the high-margin growth frontier. Why diagnostics sits awkwardly between device, pharmacy, and provider reimbursement — and why that ambiguity creates both opportunity and risk.

**Day 108 · Accreditation — Joint Commission, NCQA, URAC**

**Day 109 · Corporate Compliance Programs**

**Day 110 · Fraud, Waste & Abuse — Detection & Prevention**
