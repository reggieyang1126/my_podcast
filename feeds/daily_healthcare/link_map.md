# Healthcare Domain Podcast — Transcript Index

A ten-part podcast series walking through the structure, economics, and mechanics of the U.S. healthcare system. Each episode below links to its transcript file.

---

## [Part 0 — Prologue](part-0-prologue.md)

Orientation episode framing the central puzzle: the U.S. spends roughly $15,000 per person on healthcare yet trails peer countries on life expectancy, maternal mortality, and avoidable deaths. Introduces the three foundational concepts that the rest of the series builds on — the three-party transaction (patient, provider, payer with separate incentives), Kissick's Iron Triangle of cost, quality, and access, and the WWII-era accident that tied U.S. health insurance to employment. Sets up why American healthcare is not a broken normal market but a market with its own rules.

## [Part 1a — Foundations and Public Programs](part-1a-foundations-and-public-programs.md)

Follows the question "who's on the hook for the bill?" through one working person's life — from employer-sponsored insurance (and the ERISA shield around self-funded plans) into Medicare's four parts, Medigap versus Medicare Advantage, the Part D out-of-pocket cap, and into Medicaid's fifty-six state-run variants with managed-care delegation. Closes on the dual-eligible population — 12–13 million Americans on both Medicare and Medicaid who consume a disproportionate share of spending because two rulebooks meet at their care.

## [Part 1b — Marketplace and Payer Economics](part-1b-marketplace-and-payer-economics.md)

Examines the commercial insurance side: the ACA Marketplace and how subsidy expirations reshape the risk pool, the dominance of self-funded employer plans shielded from state regulation by ERISA, and the medical loss ratio rule that caps insurer margin and drives vertical integration. Explains why UnitedHealth/Optum, CVS/Aetna, and Humana/CenterWell now own clinics, PBMs, and analytics businesses — because the rule made owning the services insurers must buy more profitable than insuring better. Covers the recent repricing of point-solution digital health vendors.

## [Part 2 — Provider and Clinical Operations](part-2-provider-and-clinical-operations.md)

Walks one patient through the provider side and stops at each handoff to ask what is happening to the money. Covers the flip from independent physician practice to hospital and corporate employment, the site-of-service differential that makes Medicare pay 1.5–3× more for identical procedures depending on the building's billing status, and the four simultaneous jobs of a clinical note (record, bill, quality artifact, legal document). Explains why outpatient consolidation, private equity rollups, and UnitedHealth's 90,000-physician enterprise all trace back to the same arbitrage.

## [Part 3 — Revenue Cycle Management](part-3-revenue-cycle-management.md)

Traces the administrative chain that turns a clinical encounter into cash, starting with a 2 a.m. ED registrar transposing two digits on a subscriber ID. Covers financial clearance, eligibility, prior authorization, the gap between physician prose and billing codes, clinical documentation integrity, the six standardized electronic transaction types, and the role of clearinghouses — including the Change Healthcare ransomware attack of February 2024 that froze claims for one in three U.S. medical encounters. Core insight: errors are cheapest to fix at the front door and most expensive wherever they finally surface.

## [Part 4 — Care Models and Payment Reform](part-4-care-models-and-payment-reform.md)

Explains why fee-for-service still flows three of every four healthcare dollars after fifteen years of reform. Frames value-based care as a staircase rather than a switch — pay-for-performance, ACOs with shared savings, then full capitation. Covers the Triple/Quintuple Aim, the ACO benchmark "ratchet" that punished the most efficient operators, and why companies like Oak Street Health, ChenMed, and Agilon ran hot in the early 2020s and then collapsed when Medicare's 2024 risk-adjustment model down-weighted exactly the diagnosis categories the operators had leaned on.

## [Part 5a — Pharmacy](part-5a-pharmacy.md)

Walks the U.S. drug supply chain from molecule to counter. Covers the 10–15 year, billion-dollar discovery odds; the patent cliff for small molecules versus the slower biosimilar cliff for biologics (Humira as the natural experiment); and the four prices any drug has at once — AWP, WAC, ASP, and Medicare's new negotiated price. Introduces the PBM rebate game, the 340B program that runs $80B+ off the gap between sticker and reality, and why GLP-1 obesity drugs are becoming the live stress test of every dynamic in the chapter.

## [Part 5b — Devices and Diagnostics](part-5b-devices-and-diagnostics.md)

Argues that "medical device" is one word doing three jobs: implants sold per patient on physician preference, capital equipment where service contracts and consumables drive the economics, and high-volume disposables. Explains the 510(k) substantial-equivalence pathway and the predicate chain back to 1976 grandfathering — why most "new" devices have never been clinically tested. Then crosses to diagnostics, where CLIA (not FDA) governs the lab, IVDs and lab-developed tests sit in a regulatory seam, and a 2025 federal court decision recently tossed FDA's LDT rule.

## [Part 6 — Regulatory and Compliance](part-6-regulatory-and-compliance.md)

Tours the sedimentary stack of healthcare regulation through the lens of one community hospital. Covers CMS as the largest regulator that issues no licenses but writes 40% of revenue checks with conditions attached; HIPAA's privacy rule and its breach-notification "Wall of Shame" enforcement mechanism; and the three-tier fraud pyramid — Stark's strict-liability self-referral rules, the Anti-Kickback Statute, and the False Claims Act multiplier that turns paperwork errors into ten-figure cases. Explains why encryption is the single biggest free-money compliance lever in the industry.

## [Part 7 — Health IT and Data Standards](part-7-health-it-and-data-standards.md)

Asks where the $38 billion HITECH stimulus went, given that doctors still fax records. Distinguishes the EHR (longitudinal network) policy bought from the EMR (one-building chart) the industry actually installed. Walks the three messaging languages running simultaneously — HL7 v2 inside the building, FHIR APIs around it, X12 for the money — plus the underlying code-system mismatches. Covers the 2016 Cures Act information-blocking flip, the TEFCA national exchange framework, and the seam-level failures (patient matching, alert fatigue, security) where the stack actually breaks.

## [Part 8 — Population Health and Public Health](part-8-population-health-and-public-health.md)

Takes the seat of a new population health director at a rural southeast Medicaid plan. Distinguishes public health (government police power) from population health (payer contracts, screening, care management). Explains the brutal concentration of spending — top 5% drives half of all spend, bottom half drives only 3% — that makes value-based contracts mathematically possible. Covers chronic disease management, the upstream parable of social determinants of health, and the persistent Black maternal mortality gap that income and education do not explain.

## [Part 9 — Patient Experience and Access](part-9-patient-experience-and-access.md)

Frames the shift of the patient from beneficiary to buyer — high-deductible plans, HSAs, $100M Americans carrying medical debt, and the 2025 federal medical-debt credit-reporting ban that was issued and judicially vacated inside one calendar year. Covers health literacy, teach-back, the digital front door (MyChart reaching 150M+ Americans), and four structural barriers to access — geography, language, disability, and broadband. Closes on HCAHPS — the standardized hospital survey — and how Medicare turned patient experience from a marketing topic into a payment one.

## [Part 10 — Epilogue](part-10-epilogue.md)

Closing episode. Takes one ordinary industry decision — a commercial insurer tightening prior-authorization rules on GLP-1s — and traces it through all nine prior domains within a single quarter, showing that the series did not cover nine systems but one system seen from nine angles. Then looks out at the next 3–5 years and flags the three windshield items most likely to reshape the picture, starting with embedded clinical AI — ambient documentation, autonomous coding, FDA-cleared imaging algorithms quietly woven into existing workflows.
